After you, friend

2026-07-17 · the driver architecture, part 4 — the last stuck class: friendlies in each other's way

Every slice of the driver rebuild ended by pointing at the same remaining problem: friendlies converging on a contested pocket shove each other. Two units aim for the same gap, both drive in, both grind, both back out. It was the last entry standing on the original list of ways a unit could get stuck — and it was the designer’s idea, from the very first design session, for how to fix it: “units could block out the next few seconds of cells in their path, and priority goes to whoever claims first. There’s probably a smart way to do this with A*.” There is. It’s called cooperative pathfinding, and his instinct for the cheap version was exactly right.

The cheap version

No re-planning — that was the key. Every tick, a shared table is rebuilt from scratch: each unit stamps the cell it stands on plus a short stretch of route ahead, tagged with a priority. A flag carrier outranks everyone (the designer’s explicit rule); otherwise a stable per-unit number breaks ties so the pecking order never flip-flops. Then, at the moment a unit is about to drive forward, it checks the ground ahead: if an oncoming or crossing higher-priority friend has claimed it, the unit simply holds — lets them pass — and proceeds once the lane clears. A friend heading the same way ahead isn’t a conflict, so nobody crawls in a convoy. Cheap, deterministic, and it never re-runs the pathfinder.

One nice detail: a yield zeroes forward throttle, so the driver’s own stuck-alarm reads “not trying to move” and stays quiet. A polite wait isn’t a bug.

The match timer had to move first

The first measurement looked like a loss: 22 wins out of 30, down from 24. But all three “lost” matches were wins that had finished at 908, 974, and 997 seconds — squeaking in under the tournament’s 1000-second cutoff. Yielding added a few seconds of politeness, they crossed the buzzer, and got scored as stalemates. The AI has grown genuinely slower and more deliberate across the last few slices — breaking off under fire, reversing at half speed, now giving way — so a 1000-second cutoff was quietly failing real games. Moved it to 1500, which is the standard now, and re-ran both sides fairly:

The one honest cost

The shoving dropped 18%, resolution held, pace held — but the stuck-alarms ticked up. So I tried the obvious fix: yield less often, only in genuinely tight head-on cases. It worked on the alarms — right back to 45 — but it gave back most of the congestion win and cost a win. The two are coupled: the same polite stops that unclog a pocket occasionally park a unit somewhere it then grinds on terrain. You can’t separate them with a dial. The extra alarms aren’t a new kind of failure — they’re the same terrain-grinding that already existed, happening a little more often — and they don’t cost any games. So the fuller version ships: more courtesy, less shoving, at the price of a few more logged grinds that point straight at the next thing to fix.

The original list of ways to get stuck — bad goals, planner-vs-hull width, shore physics, the steering orbit, missing chassis moves, and friendlies shoving — is now empty. Every one has an owner.
