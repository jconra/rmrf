The rookie vs the table: what the net learned in one evening

2026-07-11 · the mission assigner, part 2 — training, tournament, and the turtle mystery

Part 1 ended with a promise: collect 150 matches of experience, train the 404-weight brain, and put it in the ring against the hand-written role table it wants to replace. All three happened the same day. The dataset came in at 7,189 graded tours (148 of 150 matches resolved). Training took under a minute — 53 passes over the data and the error curve flattened, 6.5% better at predicting a tour's grade than any fixed per-role average could be. The interesting part is never the error number, though. It's what the weights settled into.

What it learned — nobody wrote these

Because the net is small, we can interrogate it: freeze the battlefield at "average," sweep one input from 0 to 1, and watch where the pick flips. Three rules fell out:

  • Enemy fort healthy → warrior. Enemy fort under half → rogue. When the walls are up, send pressure; when the base is cracked, stop brawling and sneak the finish.
  • Firebrat roster full → rogue. Runners to spare means sneaky plays are affordable.
  • Teammates all warriors → deal something else. It learned army diversity.
SWEEP ONE INPUT, WATCH THE PICK FLIP — enemy fort health, everything else held average

Sensible instincts. But the sweeps also showed something suspicious: across almost every battlefield we could compose, the net refused to deal turtle (6% of picks) or hunter (4%). It had effectively fired the defenders. Before trusting it with an army, two questions: does it win anyway, and is it right about the defenders?

The tournament: 120 matches, no mercy

Same gate as every feature since post 5, doubled: net-vs-table head-to-head over 30 seeds with sides swapped (60 matches — nobody gets to blame spawn luck), plus 30 all-net and 30 all-table matches to check nothing about match resolution breaks.

Read it honestly: 32–26 is a lean, not a knockout. A first-generation net, educated by one evening of self-play, played even-to-slightly-ahead of a table we tuned by hand across eleven blog posts — while ignoring defenders entirely — and never got its flag farmed for the insolence. That last part demanded an investigation.

The turtle mystery

The designer's reaction to "the net benched the defenders" was immediate: "when I turtle I get tons of kills — defenders have tower support and easy healing." A testable claim. So we pulled every turtle tour in the dataset and compared its kill record to the warriors':

KILLS PER TOUR — GUARD DUTY vs FRONT LINE (2,611 turtle tours, 1,741 warrior tours)

The designer is right. The distributions are nearly twins — 45% of turtle tours end killless vs 41% of warrior tours, and turtles land two-plus kills almost as often (35% vs 37%). Guard duty in this game is not passive; the fights come to you, and the towers help you win them. The net did NOT bench the turtle because turtles sit idle. So why?

A 6% grade gap becomes a 94% benching

Average tour grade: warrior 0.914, turtle 0.860. Six percent. But the policy plays argmax — always the highest-scoring card, never the runner-up. It doesn't deal the second-best card a little less often; it deals it never:

THE ARGMAX AMPLIFIER — a small grade gap, a total benching

And where does the 6% come from? Look at the scorecard: kills (0.35 each), fort damage (0.9 — the jackpot), flag touches (1.2), match win (0.4), survival (0.25). Every big prize is offensive. A turtle matches the warrior kill-for-kill but never touches the fort-damage jackpot, because it's home guarding the fort instead of chewing one. Worse: a guard so intimidating that no attack ever comes logs zeros across the board — a straight F for a perfect shift. Defense's product is the attack that didn't happen, and a per-tour report card can't see a counterfactual.

One more twist from the conditionals: even with the home fort under 60% health — actively being sieged — warrior tours STILL grade above turtle tours, 0.848 to 0.813. Under this scorekeeper the mathematically best defense is a counter-attack, so that's what the net learned. The net isn't wrong. The scorekeeper is.

Verdict, and the v2 plan

The pipeline is proven — data, training, inference, tournament, all of it ran end-to-end in a day. But the net stays opt-in (deck remains the default): we don't ship a 55% lean measured by a referee that can't see half the game. The fix isn't more net. It's a fairer scorecard:

  • Pay the guards: credit a tour for own-fort HP preserved on its watch, for the flag still being home when it ends, for enemies destroyed inside our perimeter.
  • Recollect, retrain, re-gate. Fifteen more box-hours. If guards start getting dealt when guarding pays, the scorekeeper was the disease. If the net still shuns them and keeps winning — then tempo genuinely rules this game's meta, and that's a game-design finding, not an AI bug.
