The net that loved turtles too much

2026-07-12 · the mission assigner, part 3 — the fixed scorekeeper, the swing, and the wall

Part 2 ended with a plan: the scorekeeper couldn't see defense, so pay the guards — credit kills made on our own half, credit downing a flag thief, penalize a flag lost on your watch — then recollect, retrain, and re-fight the tournament. All of that happened overnight. Along the way the hand-written defenders got better too (the turtle now guards the shield generator and patches itself up between contacts; the hunter runs disruption tours — both gated +2 resolutions and shipped). Then we retrained the net on 7,672 fresh tours, every match of the collection resolving. And the net read the new scorecard very carefully.

THE PENDULUM — same net, same game, different scorekeeper

Under the offense-only card it benched the defenders (6% turtle). Under the fixed card it benched the attackers — 62% turtle, warriors down to 8%. It learned real things on the way: home fort destroyed → go rogue and steal a win; our runner is carrying their flag → field a hunter to screen the chase; teammates all guarding → deal something else. Legible, tactical, reasonable. Then it fought the hand table for 120 matches.

The net still went 59% turtle. That's the tell that this isn't a scorecard bug anymore. It's something structural.

The wall: nobody's tour buys the win

Here's the uncomfortable truth the three generations triangulate. Guard tours really do post the best per-tour numbers — tower-covered duels are the game's best kill farm (the designer has been saying this since post 13, and now it's on the books). But RMRF has exactly one victory condition: carry the flag home. A team of six excellent guards never wins anything. The scorecard grades each tour on what it produced — and the one thing that actually ends the match is produced by a handful of tours, at the very end, credited to everyone equally:

THE DILUTION — a match's ~50 tours, one flag capture, and where the win credit goes

Every tour in a winning match gets the same little win bonus — the guard who farmed four kills, the warrior who cracked the gate, the firebrat that died in a ditch at minute two. The scorecard cannot say "the gate-crack is what bought this." So the net optimizes what it can see — fat individual tours — and drifts toward an army of excellent individuals that never wins together. Grading tours one at a time has hit its ceiling.

The consolation prizes are real: the net-as-diagnostic loop is what produced the shield-guarding turtle, the harassing hunter, and the mine-kill credit fix. The hand table the net keeps losing to is a much stronger opponent than it was three days ago — because of the net. And v1 still plays even with it while fielding completely different armies, which makes it a fine opt-in variety mode.

The end boss: stop grading tours, start grading BRAINS

The fix for "we can't tell which decision won the match" is beautifully blunt: don't try. Stop scoring decisions. Score whole brains, by the only number that can't lie — did the team win the match? It works like breeding, not like homework:

  • Make a population of nets — say 20 copies with randomly nudged weights.
  • Let them fight: everyone plays matches in the headless sim (40 seconds each).
  • Selection: the nets that win survive. The ones that lose are deleted.
  • Mutation: survivors are copied with small random nudges to their 404 weights.
  • That's one generation. Repeat overnight.
EVOLUTION — twenty little brains, the winners breed, the win rate climbs

Notice what disappeared: the scorecard. No per-tour grades, no reward weights to tune, no dilution problem — a brain that deals six turtles simply loses its matches and is bred out, and nobody ever had to figure out why it lost. The win itself is the teacher. The cost is honesty of a different kind: evolution is a slow student. It needs thousands of matches — which is exactly what a headless island that plays a full war in 40 seconds is for.
