The commanders get a brain: the neural net begins

2026-07-11 · the mission assigner, part 1 — the data pipeline

Every AI improvement in this lab so far has been a rule we wrote by hand. Shoot the obstacle. Bench the mission that fails twice. Pull back when you're losing badly. Good rules — but somebody had to notice each problem, invent each fix, and tune each threshold. Post 12 is the start of something different: a piece of the AI that writes its own rules, by playing thousands of matches and keeping whatever worked.

What we picked for it to learn

Not driving. Navigation stays A* and physics — a wrong path is a bug you can see and fix, and we want it that way. The net gets the strategy layer, and one decision in particular. Since the 3v3 update, each commander fields support units with role cards dealt from a fixed table: a Warrior always brings a hunter and a home guard, a Turtle always brings two guards and a counter-puncher. Same deal, every match, no matter what's actually happening on the island. That table was always a placeholder. The learned version asks, every time a support unit redeploys: given what's happening right now — what role should this one field?

Losing towers at home with the enemy fleet still fat? Maybe deal a guard. Their base is cracked open and our roster's healthy? Deal a second warrior and push. That's the whole job: read the battlefield, pick one of four cards — warrior · turtle · hunter · rogue.

So what IS a neural network?

Strip the mystique: it's a stack of multiply-adds. You feed it a list of numbers describing the situation (ours has 21 — match clock, fleet remaining, scrap, tower counts, fort health both sides, flag status, what roles the teammates are running…). Each connection in the net has a weight — how much that input matters. Each middle "neuron" sums up its weighted inputs and fires if the total is positive. The four outputs each end up with a score, one per role, and the commander plays the highest one. Watch it think:

A FORWARD PASS — 21 battlefield numbers in, one role out (new situation every few seconds)

Every dot on the left is one fact about the battle, brighter = bigger. The lines are the weights — the knowledge lives there, not in the neurons. Untrained, those weights are random noise and the picks are nonsense. Training is the process of nudging millions of… well, in our case 404 little numbers until the picks stop being nonsense.

Where does the answer key come from?

A net learns from graded examples. Ours come from a system this blog already knows: the mission report card from post 6 — the one that grades every unit's tour of duty when it dies (kills? base damage? a hand on the flag?) and benches missions that fail twice. That grading is exactly a training label. So every tour any commander fields now becomes one row of training data, automatically:

ONE TOUR = ONE TRAINING EXAMPLE

Snapshot the 21 numbers at deploy, remember which role was fielded, let the report card grade what came back. Deploy → fight → grade → one line in the dataset. A 3v3 match produces 50–60 of these. The collector is running matches in the background as this post goes up: 150 matches, ~8,000 graded tours, about 45 seconds per match on the sim clock.

The trap: you can't learn from a deck that never changes

If we only recorded the fixed table's picks, the dataset could never answer "what if the Turtle had fielded a rogue here?" — it never happens. The net would just learn to imitate the table it's supposed to replace. So collection runs with an explore policy: 65% of tours play the normal deck, and 35% field a random role instead. Some of those random picks will be terrible. Perfect — terrible picks with bad grades are exactly how the net learns they're terrible in that situation.

How the learning actually works

Training is a loop of guess → grade → nudge. Show the net one recorded tour: it predicts how well each role should do from those 21 numbers. Compare against what the report card actually said happened. Every weight that pushed the guess in the wrong direction gets nudged the other way — a tiny amount. One example teaches it almost nothing; eight thousand examples, replayed over and over, wear grooves:

GUESS → GRADE → NUDGE — the error shrinks as the weights settle

Ground rules (so we can trust it)

  • No cheating: the 21 inputs only contain what the commander already legitimately knows — its own state plus scouted intel. The net sees the same fog of war the rules did.
  • The persona survives: slot 0 — the commander itself — always keeps its archetype. The net assigns the support missions around it. A Warrior with a learned adjutant is still a Warrior.
  • It's a knob: deck / explore / nn. Default is deck — zero behavior change until the weights earn their place.
  • The gate is the tournament: same as every feature since post 5. 30 paired seeds, net vs table, and the verdict file decides. Smarter decisions get kept; magic doesn't.
