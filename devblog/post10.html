We gave the AI landmines. It bombed itself.

2026-07-08 · Firebrat deployables (mines + sensor pods) · designed by Jacob, measured by tournament

The Firebrat is the flag runner — fragile, fast, and with nothing to do in the opening except wait for a base to be cracked. So we gave it a job: on turn one, drive out and seed the midfield with land mines and a sensor pod. Mines are dumb (they bite anyone who isn't a flyer); pods paint the nearest enemy's type and range on the screen edge. A whole new tactical layer, and the fragile little scout finally has a reason to leave the garage early.

Then we ran the tournament. Twice. The first read looked like a win — and it was lying.

The short-clock trap

First A/B: 16 seeds, a 600-second cap for fast iteration. Mines came out ahead — 9/16 resolved vs 7/16 baseline, with lower stuck. Ship it, right? No. A short clock rewards anything that speeds up the games that were already going to resolve, and hides the cost in the games that now never finish inside the window. The real budget is 1500s — that's where the 92.5% headline lives. So we re-ran at full length.

TOURNAMENT RESOLUTION — 40 seeds, 1500s cap (the real budget)

At full length the sign flips: baseline 37/40 (92.5%), with mines 34/40 (85%). Mines fixed two baseline stalemates (seeds 32, 249 — area denial broke a standoff) but caused five new ones (11, 67, 186, 242, 277). Net −3. The feature moved the headline the wrong way.

Do the mines get kills? (yes — the wrong ones)

Jacob's question, and the one worth instrumenting. So every detonation now snapshots who was alive before the blast and who died after, and tags each kill enemy or own-team. Across 40 matches the AI laid 458 mines and detonated 307 of them (67%) for 18,081 damage. Body count: 70 kills. Six were the enemy.

MINE KILLS — enemy vs. own team, 40 matches

Sixty-four of seventy. The AI mines its own outbound attack lane, then marches its own siege units and runners straight across it. On a symmetric CTF the shortest path to the enemy is the shortest path back through your own minefield — so the team most exposed to a mine is the one that laid it. The soft-steer avoidance we added (a nudge, never a hard nav block — that version boxed units in and tripled stuck before we caught it) helps a little, but a runner committed to a lane will eat the mine on it.

SEED 116
Eight own goals

The clearest case: one match laid 23 mines, detonated 19, and killed 8 of its own units — zero enemies — for 1,200 self-inflicted damage. It did not resolve. A commander slowly demining itself, one friendly vehicle at a time.

DIAGNOSIS: dumb mines on your own advance route are a self-attrition engine.

The feedback loop

There's a second tell in the per-seed data: the matches that stalemate lay far more mines than the ones that resolve. Partly that's just time — a longer game sends more Firebrat trips, and each trip lays two more mines. But it runs both ways: more mines means more friendly casualties, which means a weaker attack, which means a longer game, which means more mines.

What this buys us

The player-facing feature is genuinely good — you deploy mines and pods from the Firebrat, the pod's contact readout is legit intel, and a Valkyrie rocket clears a mine on a ground hit. The AI's use of it is what backfired, and the telemetry says exactly why: dumb mines want to be laid where the enemy crosses, not where you do. The fixes the data points to, in order of bluntness:

  • Owner-safe mines — a mine ignores the team that laid it (still dumb to everyone else). Kills the 91% at a stroke; costs the "either team" purity of the original rule.
  • Defensive placement — lay on the inbound approaches to your own base (where attackers come), not on your outbound attack lane. Keeps mines dumb; needs the sapper to think about where the enemy will be, not where it's going.
  • Fewer, later — cap mines hard and stop the runner-trip drip that feeds the loop.

That's the next build. For now the honest scoreboard: a fun new layer that, left dumb and laid forward, turned the AI into its own most effective sapper.
