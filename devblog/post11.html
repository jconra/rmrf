The army got a repair truck. Nobody's allowed to shoot it.

2026-07-10 · tower repair crews + gun purchases · designed by Jacob, measured by tournament

New logistics layer: each base parks three jeeps in a motor pool out back. When a corner tower gets chewed up, a jeep rolls out with a three-man construction crew, parks under the tower, and heals it at 1% per second — a scratch takes seconds, a half-wrecked tower takes the minute, and if it keeps taking fire mid-repair the crew just stays longer. The rules split clean: masonry is free (the crew's exposed minutes are the cost), hardware costs scrap — a replacement tower gun runs 3 scrap, rides out on the jeep, and is lost with it. Even a tower blown to full rubble can be raised again — it rebuilds bottom-up, each course re-seating in exact reverse of how it crumbled, because both directions read the same authored fall thresholds.

The AI buys a gun only from a position of strength (10+ scrap banked, 5+ vehicles left), and its sortie timing is a random 5–45s draw so you can't camp a wounded tower and pick off crews on a metronome. Then we ran the 30-seed tournament with counters on everything.

What the crews actually did

Across 30 full-map matches the two teams launched 118 sorties (3.9 per match; the busiest game ran 8). 86 completed and drove home to re-park. 15 were shot off the road mid-job. The rest were still out working when the flag fell. And the headline inside the headline: 50 of those jobs were full rubble resurrections — a tower that had been flattened to debris, raised course by course while the fight went on around it.

118 SORTIES, 30 MATCHES — HOW THEY ENDED

The gun run is a real gamble

Teams bought 19 replacement guns (57 scrap). Only 11 made it onto a tower. The other 8 died in transit — the jeep was destroyed carrying the cargo, scrap gone, no refund. That's a 42% loss rate on gun deliveries, which makes the purchase decision genuinely interesting: the AI is rich when it buys, but the delivery window is the most dangerous 30 seconds in the game.

REPLACEMENT GUNS — BOUGHT vs DELIVERED

Now the twist: nobody was aiming at them

Jeeps took 63 hits and lost 32 of their number — 15 on the road, 17 raided in the parked lot. Sounds like a hunted species. Except we checked the AI's targeting code: its shot selection draws from exactly three lists — enemy vehicles, siege structures (walls, tower guns, the HQ), and obstacles blocking its path. Jeeps are in none of them. A commander cannot decide to shoot a repair truck.

every one of those 63 hits was stray fire — siege shells splashing into the motor pool, tower crossfire, misses walking through a work site. the most-shot-at unit in the game has never once been aimed at.

Which means the jeep-hunting layer — the whole "if you're worried about their repairs, target the jeeps" counterplay the system was designed around — currently only exists for the human player. You can hunt trucks. The AI can't. And even so, blind splash alone kills a third of the fleet's deliveries. When commanders learn to hunt deliberately (the Hunter and Rogue personas are built for exactly this), the escort problem becomes real strategy.

Did free repairs stall the game?

The worry with any healing mechanic in a flag-only-win game: infinite repair = infinite siege. Jacob's counter when we designed it — scrap is finite, so only a team that already won the economy can afford to keep its towers alive, and that team was winning anyway. The A/B agrees:

THE INVISIBLE TRUCK
A strategy layer only one side can play

The system works: crews repair, guns get delivered, the economy prices it, the match still resolves. But its central tension — protect your jeeps, hunt theirs — is one-sided until the AI can see a truck as a target.

NEXT: give Hunter/Rogue commanders a truck-hunting appetite, give the Turtle an escort habit, and re-run the tournament to see what deliberate interdiction does to those 42%-risk gun runs.

UPDATE, same day: we taught them to shoot the truck

Jacob's call on the design: no hunting mission — "I'd just kill them if I see them. Otherwise I worry it could distract them from winning the game." So it shipped as a target of opportunity only: a ground gunner with an idle gun and no vehicle engaged pot-shots any enemy jeep in its range, arc and line of sight. It never steers toward one.
