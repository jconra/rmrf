One number per plan

2026-07-17 · the blueprint for MissionScore — a fight-or-flight for strategy

The AI already has a number that decides every fight: the fight-or-flight score, a stack of weighted terms — my hp, my ammo, the matchup, the numbers, who has whose back — that adds up to a single signed value. Positive, fight; negative, run. It works because it’s one place where every consideration meets.

Strategy has never had that. The commander picks its mission from a cascade of if-statements and a persona playbook, and its only memory of failure is a crude “ban a mission after two strikes.” So a winning team can throw the same doomed flag-runner up the same guarded lane eight times in a row (that really happened — a match where the flag sat grabbable for eighteen minutes while the runner died and died and died). The designer’s rule for the fix is one line: the worst thing you can do is repeat a mistake.

So: MissionScore — a fight-or-flight for strategy. Every mission gets a weight from the same board state; the commander runs the highest one. It’s the job we hoped the neural net would learn (it couldn’t beat the hand-tuned table), written out by hand — and, like the fight weights before it, a candidate to be evolved later.

How it reads the board

  • Every mission is scored, including the emergencies. “Chase the flag thief” isn’t a special override — it’s just the Intercept mission, whose weight spikes the instant our flag is picked up.
  • Events force a re-think. Flag exposed, flag stolen, flag dropped, a unit lost — each forces an immediate re-score instead of waiting out the current mission’s dwell, so the commander snaps to what just changed.
  • The mission picks its own vehicle. We score ~14 missions, not 14×4 mission-and-vehicle pairs. Which ride to send is the mission’s call; what we have feeds the weight.
  • Never repeat a failure. Every mission carries a success value: it drops to −4 when the mission fails and drifts back toward 0 a little each decision — so a just-failed plan is suppressed, then slowly forgiven once something else works.

Success  0 normally → drops to −4 the moment a mission fails, then +1 back toward 0 each decision. The anti-repeat memory — a plan that just died gets benched, then forgiven once something else lands.

Affordability  −6 if we can’t field or afford the mission’s vehicle at all (a Capture with no runner and no scrap for one). Keeps the scorer honest about what’s actually on the lot.

The fleet-comp term, in full

Play to your strength. Each vehicle type, once it’s a big enough share of the fleet, pushes the mission it’s built for. The push scales: (type’s share of the fleet − its threshold) × 10, bonus-only.

  • Firebrat → Capture  (threshold 0.40)
  • Lurcher → Attack  (0.23)
  • Valkyrie → Siege-back  (0.15)
  • Jotun → Siege-front  (0.15)

Example — a fleet of 2 Firebrats and 2 Jotun: Jotun is half the fleet, so Siege-front gets (0.50 − 0.15) × 10 = +3.5; Firebrat is also half, so Capture gets (0.50 − 0.40) × 10 = +1.0. A heavy-Jotun army naturally leans on the front siege; a runner-heavy one leans on the grab — and it slides smoothly as the fleet takes losses.
