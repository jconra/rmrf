The pecking order

2026-07-23 · four personalities, 320 matches, and the tax on getting stuck

The commander runs one of four doctrines — Turtle, Warrior, Hunter, Rogue — dealt at random each match. They’ve fought thousands of games, but never in a fair bracket: a random draw tangles a persona’s record up with whatever maps and matchups it happened to catch. So we ran the bracket properly. Every ordered pairing, both sides of every map so side/spawn luck cancels, 20 seeds each — 320 deterministic matches — tracking three things per persona: who wins, how long it takes, and how often its units get stuck (driver nav alarms).

There is a little wheel among the other three — hunter owns warrior (70%), warrior edges turtle, turtle and hunter split down the middle. But rogue sits on top of the whole thing at 60–68%. The rear-siege, flank-and-sneak playstyle is simply the strongest thing in the game right now.

The surprise is in the last column

The ranking is one thing. The real find is the alarms column. Line the four up by win rate and their stuck-rate falls into a clean ladder — every step down in wins is a step up in alarms:

rogue   60.6%   0.10 stuck
hunter  51.3%     0.14 stuck
turtle   44.4%     0.17 stuck
warrior 42.5%   0.23 stuck

The doctrines that drive units into walls lose; the ones that flank around trouble win. Warrior — the “charge the front” doctrine — gets stuck more than twice as often as rogue and finishes dead last despite being the aggressive one. Its win rate isn’t losing to better tactics so much as to navigation: a unit that alarms out mid-siege isn’t fighting. Rogue’s edge is partly that its rear approaches just don’t pile units into the same choke.

That lines up with a thread we’ve been pulling all week — the stuck-unit hunt, and the release valve for siegers parked outside their own gun’s range. This bracket says that work isn’t just tidiness; it’s win rate, and the most navigation-heavy doctrine has the most to gain from it.

The honest catch

Rogue at 60.6% across the board is over-tuned. A healthy roster wants every doctrine to have a real shot; right now picking rogue is close to picking “win.” That’s a balance lever for another day — probably reining in the rear-siege / stealth-capture edge, then re-running this exact bracket to check the wheel evens out.

Meanwhile, above ground

A lighter note from the same week: the island’s grass got a few cheap upgrades aimed at making the field feel alive. Blades now fade from a grass-green root into a darker tip, so a patch reads as a continuous field instead of scattered clumps. The whole canopy — grass, bushes, trees, palm fronds — sways in a wind that travels across the map as a wave rather than shifting in lockstep (one uniform per frame; the GPU does the rest). And the blades only grow where the grass texture actually shows, not on the sandy patches inside a grass tile.

The rabbit hole, as always, was performance. A grass blade was a 12-triangle cone — but it turns out a single flat triangle reads just as grassy at field density, a 12× cut. Rather than argue about it, there’s now a live tuning tool: the Grass Lab — sliders for density, blade shape (cone / spike / tapered quad / camera-facing billboard), height, colour, and wind, with a running triangle-and-fps count so the performance cost is never a guess.
