The brain that beat the brain

2026-07-18 · MissionScore ships — five tuning rounds, one dethroned cascade, and a lesson about shiny scoreboards

Post 19 was the blueprint: score every mission from the board state, run the highest, never repeat a failure. This post is what happened when the table met the incumbent — the hand-tuned playbook cascade, ~300 builds of accumulated fixes, the brain that has run every match this game has ever played.

Two arenas

Because both brains live in one build behind a switch, the new one had to win twice. Mirror matches (new brain on both sides): is the game still healthy? And head-to-head (one brain per team, every seed played twice with sides swapped): who actually wins? The first draft lost the fight badly — 26–34 — and every loss taught a named lesson, because every decision now logs its arithmetic:

The reroute, live

The signature moment, from a real match log — the seed where the old brain once threw eight straight runners up the same guarded lane for eighteen minutes:

734s CYAN: The flag’s wide open — send the runner, grab it and RUN!
  ↳ capture-right +11 (flag OPEN +4, grabbable +2, towers down right +1, lane clear +1, spare FB +1, fleet F +2)
  > capture-left +10  > capture-front +9 (… lane blocked −2 …)

The front lane carries a known interceptor — scored down. The right lane was scouted clear — scored up. The runner goes right and wins the match. And the intel is honest: lanes are only “clear” if our units actually had eyes on them; an unscouted lane is unknown, so scouting earns confidence instead of the commander peeking through the fog.

The chaos-shine lesson

Round 3’s 35–22 looked like a knockout. It wasn’t reproducible: two more measurements of near-identical builds came back 30–29 and 31–28 — and one of those builds produced a mirror board byte-identical to its predecessor while the head-to-head still moved. Deterministic matches make each measurement exact, but comparisons between builds still reshuffle ±4–5 games of pure chaos. A shiny scoreboard is not a mandate; the honest claim is “slightly ahead, never behind, across every measurement.”

MissionScore is now the default brain. The cascade stays in the build behind the switch — a permanent baseline, and a reminder. The neural net couldn’t beat the cascade (post 14); the designer’s weight table did. Turns out the winning move was making the judgment legible — a table a person can read, argue with, and tune in an afternoon — and then letting the logs name every mistake.
