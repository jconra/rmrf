One driver per vehicle

2026-07-16 · the driver architecture, part 1 — a failed refactor, a design session, and 2,579 confessions

This one starts with a question from the designer that turned out to be the whole post: “How come there are so many steering programs? Can’t it just be built with layers of abstraction so that travel, siege-standoff and combat all call the same function for steering?”

He was right to ask. Travel steering, siege approach, duel footwork, the recall trip home, the gate exit — each carried its own inline copy of “turn toward the thing, drive at the thing,” and every copy re-grew the same diseases on its own schedule. The famous spinning Lurcher was one of them: full throttle plus full turn around a goal inside the turning circle is a stable orbit, and three separate steering copies were each capable of producing it.

The experiment that half-worked

First attempt: extract the ONE steering primitive (locomote(kin, order) — eased throttle so a misaligned hull pivots instead of orbiting; omni chassis translate immediately in any direction) and port the existing states onto it. The math was provably right — unit tests, a forced-strafe probe showing the Jotun’s illegal combat sidestep going from 21.3u to 0u, the orbit class dead. The tournament said otherwise:

The primitive fixed the orbit and created a new stuck class in the same breath — because the wiring was still the old spaghetti: five call sites each deciding when steering applies, what the goal is, and who owns the heading. Verdict file says PARK. The math survived; the wiring was condemned.

The design session

Instead of iterating on wiring we were about to replace anyway, the designer specified the real thing: “the vehicle just has a single driver. The driver knows how to go forward, reverse, rotate, and strafe if it’s not a Jotun… we don’t want a bunch of drivers waiting to jump in the seat depending on the situation.” And the line that became the architecture’s conscience: “I actually don’t even like stuck/jolt recovery. I feel like that should never happen. We don’t have mud in the game. If stuck happens then something failed.”

So: stuck is a bug report, not a situation. The full spec lives in 2026-07-16-driver-architecture.md next to this post; the short version is four layers —

And when movement fails anyway, no recovery dance: an ALARM — flight-recorder dump, a counter that shames us in every tournament, and if the unit is still pinned after a grace period it scuttles itself. A bug should cost the team, loudly. That’s the incentive structure for fixing root causes instead of apologizing for them.

Slice 1: the driver takes the seat

Shipped today: js/Driver.js. Travel states now issue standing GOTO orders; the driver route-follows through the same A* cache and drives through the locomote core. Combat footwork still steers itself under a DIRECT order — the driver observes everything (flight recorder, watchdog) but doesn’t grab the wheel mid-duel. That’s a later slice. The chain of command became a thing you can just read:

WARRIOR                              ← doctrine
ATTACK · step=defend                ← mission
tactical: lost sight → re-acquire   ← commander
maneuver: GOTO(-39,65) 62% · 18u left  ← the standing order
driver:   fwd 1.0 · left 0.3          ← pedals this tick

2,579 confessions

The best part of the slice is the honesty rule: a GOTO whose A* route ends short of the goal is a contract violation — the order asked for somewhere unreachable, and before today the unit walked the partial path silently and ground against whatever blocked the last leg. First 30-match tournament with the contract being checked:

nav ALARMS: 2 (0.07/match) · unreachable-GOTO contract violations: 2,579

Eighty-six impossible orders per match. The commanders have been lying to the drivers this whole time, and every silent partial path was a stuck report waiting to happen. That number is the to-do list for the next slice — each violation names its issuer.

The same tournament found the next wiring bug within the hour: the three longest stuck episodes (182s/172s/116s) were all return-to-base Lurchers — the recall path was feeding omni pedals (forward + sideways, assuming an independently-swinging nose) through the classic tank drive, which turns the hull while strafing. That mismatch is a circling machine. And the watchdog missed them, because an orbiting unit covers plenty of ground — it just never gets anywhere. Two fixes: one chassis-correct drive boundary both call sites share, and the watchdog now measures progress toward the goal, not motion. An orbit is officially a form of standing still.

Epilogue (next day): three tournament rounds and two autopsies later — the watchdog learned to catch orbits (moving without closing), creepers (0.3u/s wall-grinds), order-churn stall laundering, and the partial-path freeze. Final board, pooled across two 30-seed sets: resolution 49/60 → 50/60, wins ~50s faster, transit-stuck −11%, the 182s/172s legacy creepers dead, alarms at 0.52/match with a flight recording behind every one. The metric from here: alarms per match → zero — not by silencing the alarm, but by running out of bugs. Spec: 2026-07-16-driver-architecture.md · verdict: VERDICT-2026-07-17-driver-slice1.txt.
