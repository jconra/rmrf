The ghost in the instrument

2026-07-17 · the driver architecture, part 3 — the duel becomes orders, and a bisection from hell

Slice 3 was supposed to be the victory lap: port the duel footwork — the strafing circles, the kite-to-cover, the Valkyrie’s new jousting runs — onto driver orders, gate it, ship it. The port went fine. Then the gate said the Valkyrie’s joust cost four resolutions, the isolation knob said the joust was innocent, and the investigation that followed produced the most haunted sequence of measurements this project has seen:

  1. Turning the joust OFF didn’t bring the lost seeds back. Fine — something else.
  2. A byte-level bisection: stripping provably-dead code flipped match outcomes.
  3. The dead branch, instrumented with a counter: zero executions. Outcome still flipped.
  4. The same configuration, run twice: two different answers.

Step 4 was the tell. The “impossible bisection” was built on a ghost run — the deterministic instrument from post 16 is deterministic almost always. Async asset loading consumes a load-order-dependent slice of the seeded RNG stream, and under IO contention that order occasionally swaps: same seed, silently different match, roughly one boot in ten. Every anomaly in the sequence above was one bad measurement propagating through honest logic.

The fix: RR.reseed(seed) — re-pin the RNG stream at drive-start, after loading has consumed whatever it wants. Seed 11, hammered four times under deliberate parallel contention: byte-identical. Third determinism leak dead (the commander draw, the rAF frames, now this).

What actually shipped

  • ORBIT — the strafe duel as an order: circle at the range band, nose on the rival (or on a fleer’s lead point, so pressing cuts the corner), radius kept honest by the driver instead of drifting into kill arcs. The Jotun-ambush blind-rear circle uses it too.
  • KITE — kite-to-cover, the trap lure, and the siege break-off unified: an A*-routed retreat, gun on the threat the whole way. Three versions to get right — the first backed blind into terrain, the second re-invented the omni-pedals-through-tank-drive disease — the third folds desired motion into the hull frame around whatever the nose is doing. Doctrine note: a tank now retreats gun-first at half throttle instead of showing its back.
  • JOUST — the Valkyrie’s signature, from the designer’s own spec: no more hover-duels; a full-speed pass with the target held ~14u abeam, missiles through the broadside window, extend, reassess. The driver plans each pass and alternates sides on its own — the brain just keeps saying “joust him,” and the approach serpentines.
  • Collision response — the Layer-0 competency the spec promised: a blocked orbit circles the other way; a blocked kite dodges its bearing, alternating sides, wider each try.

The fights themselves are the healthiest ever measured. The two red numbers share one cause: congestion. Units that circle, kite, and joust sweep far more ground than units that stood and traded — and in contested siege pockets they shove each other, which the driver dutifully alarms about. That is the last root cause standing from the original stuck catalog: friendly right-of-way. The designer’s time-window path-reservation idea has been queued since the first design session. It’s next — and for the first time, we head into a slice with an instrument that has survived three rounds of adversarial debugging and a game that explains every one of its own failures.
