The instrument was lying

2026-07-17 · the driver architecture, part 2 — a morning of chasing ghosts, and the machine that ended it

This post is a confession with a happy ending. Slice 2 of the driver architecture went in — the grind detector, the reverse doctrine (every chassis backs up at half throttle, the six-legged Lurcher exempt), the siege break-off, and the “handle no” reflexes that react when the driver refuses an unreachable order. Then the tournaments started disagreeing with each other, and I spent a morning fixing “regressions” that moved every time I looked at them: 26 resolved, then 24, then 23, then 21 — each run a fresh diagnosis, each diagnosis a fresh fix, each fix another decline.

So we ran the control experiment that should have come first: the same build, twice.

identical code · identical seeds → 21/30 resolved vs 23/30 · idle-at-goal 1150 vs 2133 (±46%)

The instrument was lying. Every tournament verdict this project has ever produced sat on a noise floor of ±2–3 resolutions — including the gates that shipped features, and the “regressions” that morning was spent chasing.

Finding the leaks

A checksum probe (positions hashed every 100 ticks, two runs diffed) found the divergence at tick zero: the same seed drew turtle vs warrior in one run and hunter vs warrior in the other. Same map, different war. The commander draw ran off unseeded Math.random — and even after pinning it, a wall-clock-random number of requestAnimationFrame frames advanced the sim before and during the driven test loop, draining the RNG stream by a different amount every run.

The embarrassing part: the game already had the cure. ?dseed=N (seeded doctrine draw) and ?rngseed=N (seeded global RNG) had been sitting in main.js — the test rigs just never passed them. Add the rig-side kit (clock frozen and rAF dead from t=0, so the driven loop is the only clock) and:

two full 30-seed tournaments → byte-identical output · per-seed diffable · bisection on flipped seeds in ~12 min

What the exact instrument found in one afternoon

  • The proximity break-off was killing sieges. The doctrine says a sieger reacts when it’s being fired on; the implementation reacted to a tower being nearby — and a defended base always has an enemy nearby. Deterministic replay of one flipped seed: towers untouched for 1000s, kills tripled, every siege dissolved into field duels. Trigger rewired to actual tower hits (last 2.5s) → 4 of 8 flipped seeds recovered immediately.
  • The honesty rule was convicting the innocent. A* returns partial paths for two very different reasons: the goal is provably unreachable (search space exhausted) or the search ran out of budget (goal may be fine, just far). They shared one label, and the handlers acted on both — writing off reachable contacts, rotating away good firing positions. The path now carries budgetHit; the driver convicts only on real evidence. Honest violation count: ~10/match, not ~29.
  • The first standoff search walked into crossfire. Rotating the siege stand 0.7 rad per step in one direction found reachable ground — inside the other towers’ arcs. Reachable graves. The search now alternates small steps around the ideal line, capped at ±1.3 rad.

One seed apart at the honest horizon — inside the ±4–5-seed reshuffle that any behavior change causes (determinism buys exactness, not attribution: four of slice 1’s own stalemates resolve under slice 2). The real, systematic cost is pace: units that break off under fire, reverse at half throttle, and search for reachable firing positions take longer to win. The house rule stands — smarter decisions beat win-rate — so slice 2 ships with its slower, saner endgames, and the tournament horizon gets revisited instead.
